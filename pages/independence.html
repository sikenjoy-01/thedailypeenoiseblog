June 12, 1898 — Philippine Independence Declared

General Emilio Aguinaldo proclaimed the independence of the Philippines from Spain in Kawit, Cavite. The Philippine flag was unfurled and the national anthem was played publicly for the first time.

August 30, 1896 — Cry of Pugad Lawin

The Katipunan, led by Andrés Bonifacio, tore their cedulas as a symbol of defiance, marking the beginning of the Philippine Revolution.

March 29, 1901 — Capture of Emilio Aguinaldo

Aguinaldo was captured in Palanan, Isabela, effectively weakening organized resistance during the Philippine-American War.

February 15, 1889 — First Issue of La Solidaridad

Filipino reformists José Rizal, Marcelo H. del Pilar, and Graciano López Jaena published the first issue of La Solidaridad in Madrid. They demanded equal rights, representation in Spain, and an end to friar abuses, sparking the rise of Filipino nationalism.

THE ROAD TO PHILIPPINE INDEPENDENCE
By Ken Joshua Infante
“Manila – Early 1900s to 1935”

The journey toward freedom was long and arduous. After centuries under Spanish rule and then shifting to American control following the 1898 Treaty of Paris, Filipinos began to organize politically to reclaim their nation. The early 1900s saw leaders like Manuel Quezon, Sergio Osmeña, and others rise as champions of self-rule.

The Philippine Organic Act of 1902 established a Philippine Assembly, the first elected body where Filipinos could legislate, though under American oversight. In 1916, the Jones Law promised eventual independence and created a fully elected Philippine Legislature, galvanizing the nationalist movement.

I walked Manila’s streets during these years, hearing the fervor in speeches and debates that echoed through newly built halls of governance. Political parties like the Nacionalista Party emerged, pushing for autonomy while navigating the delicate relationship with the United States.

The 1934 Tydings-McDuffie Act was a pivotal moment as it laid out a 10-year Commonwealth period as a transition to full sovereignty. The following year, the Philippine Commonwealth was inaugurated with Manuel Quezon as president, a symbol of hope and Filipino aspiration. Yet the path ahead was uncertain, marked by the challenge of nation-building amid global turmoil.

THE COMMONWEALTH PERIOD
By Jesronjohn Quimson
“Manila – 1935 to 1946”

The Commonwealth era was a period of ambitious reforms and cultural revival. Established in 1935, the new government sought to prepare the Philippines for full independence by strengthening its political institutions, economy, and society.

President Manuel Quezon championed social justice, land reform, and the promotion of the Filipino language to foster unity among diverse ethnic groups. Educational reforms expanded access and emphasized Filipino heritage. Infrastructure projects modernized cities and connected provinces.

But the dream was soon overshadowed by the clouds of World War II. In December 1941, the Japanese invasion shattered the Commonwealth’s progress. Manila was declared an open city but still suffered intense bombings and occupation.

The Commonwealth government retreated into exile in the United States, where Quezon and later Sergio Osmeña worked tirelessly to maintain Filipino legitimacy and garner Allied support. Meanwhile, Filipinos endured brutal occupation, with resistance fighters rising across the archipelago.

Despite the interruption, the Commonwealth period laid critical foundations for governance, identity, and independence that would be reclaimed after the war.

POST WORLD WAR II RECOVERY
By Jesronjohn Quimson
“Manila and Beyond – 1946 and Beyond”

The scars of war were everywhere. Manila was one of the most devastated cities in Asia, reduced to rubble after the Battle of Manila in 1945. Millions of Filipinos faced displacement, hunger, and the challenge of rebuilding a shattered nation.

On July 4, 1946, the Philippines was finally recognized as an independent republic under the Treaty of Manila, ending nearly 50 years of American sovereignty. I stood amidst the jubilant crowds at the inauguration, witnessing a nation reclaim its future.

Reconstruction was a colossal task. The government focused on rebuilding infrastructure, revitalizing agriculture, and rehabilitating war refugees. The United States provided aid through the Bell Trade Act and the Philippine Rehabilitation Act, though these came with economic strings attached.

Political leaders worked to implement land reform and expand education, but challenges like poverty, insurgency, and economic dependency persisted. Still, the Filipino people’s resilience was undeniable.

The postwar years were both hopeful and complex which was a time of renewal, where independence was no longer a dream but a demanding reality to be shaped by the sacrifices and vision of those who endured.
